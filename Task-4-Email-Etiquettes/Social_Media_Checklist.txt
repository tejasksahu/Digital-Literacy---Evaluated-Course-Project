Social Media Do’s and Don’ts for College Students
________________________________________
DO’S
1.	Keep your profiles professional – Use a clear, appropriate profile photo and write a bio that reflects your academic and professional identity.
2.	Think before you post – Ask yourself: “Would I be comfortable if my professor, future employer, or family saw this?” If not, don’t post it.
3.	Use privacy settings – Set personal social accounts (Instagram, Snapchat) to private so only trusted connections can see your content.
4.	Engage constructively – Share helpful articles, comment thoughtfully on peers’ work, and contribute positively to academic or professional discussions.
5.	Separate personal and professional platforms – Use LinkedIn and GitHub for professional networking; keep casual content on personal accounts with strong privacy settings.
6.	Cite sources when sharing – If you share someone else’s work, idea, or image, credit the original creator to avoid plagiarism issues online.
7.	Report abusive content – If you witness cyberbullying or harassment, use the platform’s reporting tools and inform a trusted adult or college authority.
________________________________________
DON’TS
1.	Don’t post offensive, discriminatory, or inflammatory content – Comments that target individuals or groups based on religion, gender, caste, or community can have serious academic and legal consequences.
2.	Don’t share personal information publicly – Avoid posting your phone number, home address, college ID number, or daily routine on public platforms.
3.	Don’t share unverified news or rumours – Spreading misinformation — even unknowingly — can damage your reputation and harm others.
4.	Don’t engage in arguments or call-outs publicly – If you have a disagreement with someone, address it privately or through appropriate channels, not on a public timeline.
5.	Don’t post photos or information about others without their consent – Sharing images of classmates or friends without permission can violate their privacy and trust.
6.	Don’t use social media during exams or assessments – This risks academic integrity issues, especially if any course-related content is discussed.
7.	Don’t ignore suspicious messages or friend requests – Unsolicited contact from unknown profiles may be phishing attempts or social engineering attacks.
